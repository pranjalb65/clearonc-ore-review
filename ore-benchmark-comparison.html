ORE: benchmark and competitor comparison

How the comparable oncology AI products actually benchmark themselves, and where ORE can win. Source: an adversarial audit of public claims and the 2026 benchmark literature.

For the Kamal review
The one thing to lead with

The entire oncology-AI field markets accuracy, but almost nobody publishes reproducible treatment-recommendation accuracy. Headline numbers are mostly saturated exam scores, self-reported curated subsets, or satisfaction surveys relabeled as validation. ORE's wedge is to be the engine that publishes reproducible, independently checkable scores on the real benchmarks, with citations and honest weaknesses, in a market that does not.

Honest caveat to state plainly: there is still no widely adopted, peer-reviewed, public benchmark for oncology treatment recommendation. MedGUIDE and MTBBench are the closest and both are under a year old. So tumor-board concordance is best run as a prospective in-house metric, with the public benchmarks as the comparable anchors.

Where ORE wins: the seven-axis eval, deterministic engine vs a raw LLM

The 2026 evidence (Mass General Brigham, JAMA Network Open, 21 frontier models) is the backdrop: models give the right final answer over 90% of the time but fail the reasoning (differential) 80 to 100% of the time. Clinical deployment needs auditability and safety, which is exactly the trade a deterministic, cited, abstaining engine makes.

The defensible framing: ORE trades some open-ended breadth for auditability and safety, exactly the trade the 2026 evidence says clinical deployment requires. We can publish reproducible numbers on the safety and concordance axes that the field markets but does not measure, and we report our weaknesses like the one credible academic group does. That is a position no competitor currently holds.

Talking points for the call

  • The market sells accuracy numbers that do not survive scrutiny. We will win on verifiability, not on a bigger number.
  • We benchmark publicly against MedGUIDE, CancerGUIDE, MTBBench, and NOHARM, and we report where we fall short.
  • Our architecture (deterministic core, every claim cited, abstains when unsure) is built for the exact failure mode the 2026 literature identifies: reasoning and safety, not recall.
  • Retire the unsupported "99.8%". Replace it with reproducible scores and the transparent-reporting posture that clinical buyers and regulators actually trust.
