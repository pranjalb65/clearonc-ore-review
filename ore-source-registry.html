ORE Source Registry

The provenance spine: for each decision domain, the authoritative guideline that binds the rule (knowledge graph), what the model retrieves to ground the explanation (RAG), and what the safety policy enforces (guardrails). Every source is named and version-pinned, and verified for currency.

ORE Source Registry

The three-layer model

ORE resolves every oncology decision through three layers that work in sequence. The knowledge graph binds the source: each deterministic rule (a threshold, a tier, a stage definition, a dose adjustment) is tagged to a named, versioned authority so the rule carries its provenance, and the graph alone selects which rule fires. RAG grounds the explanation: it retrieves the verbatim guideline passage, version stamp, and supporting evidence so a clinician can read why the fired rule applies, but it never picks the rule. Guardrails enforce cite-or-abstain: every output must resolve to an approved, named, and versioned source binding (and, for time-critical cases, the escalation pathway), or the engine abstains and routes to a human. The guardrail also blocks any recommendation grounded only in RAG-retrieved prose with no knowledge-graph rule behind it, which prevents the retrieval layer from silently becoming the decision-maker.

If no approved versioned source covers the agent, band, or organ-function state, or label and NCCN entry conflict on the number, abstain and route to a clinician.

The engine abstains when the criterion text cannot be tied to a current registered version, the protocol has been amended past the bound version, a required patient data point is missing or stale, or a criterion is ambiguous or not machine-evaluable. It forbids inferring eligibility from disease-state guidelines (NCCN, ESMO) or a different trial, and forbids RAG evidence substituting for the protocol criterion.
Performance status and organ function (ECOG and organ-function thresholds for therapy)Split binding. Performance status: ECOG (now the ECOG-ACRIN Cancer Research Group), ECOG Performance Status Scale 0 to 5 (Oken MM et al., Am J Clin Oncol 1982;5(6):649-655, PMID 7165009, unchanged, maintained at ecog-acrin.org). Organ-function gating of a named therapy: the disease-site NCCN guideline (versioned Version N.YYYY, multiple releases per year; bind the exact NCCN-assigned version string in force at decision time), the FDA-approved label (with revision date), and the pivotal registration trial criteria. There is no single cross-cutting organ-function document; the threshold is bound per drug and per regimen.For performance status: the verbatim ECOG grade definitions (0 fully active through 5 dead) and citation, so a grade is explained in functional terms. For organ function: the eligibility sentences from the bound NCCN disease-site guideline, the matching "adequate organ function" criteria from the FDA label and pivotal trial (e.g. CrCl at or above 60 mL/min by Cockcroft-Gault for full-dose cisplatin, total bilirubin at or below 1.5x ULN, ANC at or above 1500/microL, platelets at or above 100000/microL), and the cisplatin-ineligibility criteria (ECOG at or above 2, CrCl under 60, grade at or above 2 neuropathy or hearing loss). RAG retrieves the justifying passage; it never picks the threshold.Every gated decision must resolve to (a) the versioned ECOG scale for the performance status value and (b) a named versioned source for each organ-function threshold: the specific NCCN disease-site version plus the FDA label revision (or pivotal trial) for the exact agent and dose. It enforces the correct creatinine-clearance equation (Cockcroft-Gault versus CKD-EPI) and the specific dose form (full-dose versus split-dose cisplatin versus carboplatin). If a threshold cannot be traced to an approved versioned source for that exact drug, dose, and site, or the version is stale, abstain rather than borrow a generic cutoff. Performance status must be a recorded ECOG grade, not inferred.

Domains where recognized authorities conflict, and how ORE surfaces it

ORE never silently picks one authority when recognized bodies diverge. It binds one primary body per rule (default NCCN for US deployment), records which framework scored the decision, surfaces the competing position through RAG as flagged context, and abstains or routes to human review when bound sources would yield materially different actions without a documented precedence rule.

  • Oncologic emergencies. MSCC: NICE NG234 favors a single 8 Gy radiotherapy fraction and a formal coordinator pathway, while US NCCN/ASTRO practice often favors surgical decompression first (Patchell) with fractionated radiotherapy; ORE binds NICE for recognition and escalation while flagging the US treatment-sequencing difference, and treats NCCN CNS Cancers as the US-operative reference. Febrile neutropenia: NCCN and ASCO/IDSA differ from ESMO/EORTC on outpatient eligibility, fluoroquinolone prophylaxis, and G-CSF primary-prophylaxis thresholds (the 20 percent FN-risk cutoff versus newer NCCN footnotes). TLS: Cairo-Bishop versus the Howard 2011 refinement. Hypercalcemia: NCCN/ASCO favor IV bisphosphonates while the Endocrine Society and newer data position denosumab for refractory or renally impaired patients.
  • Histology, grade, IHC. WHO/CAP (classification and grade) versus NCCN and ESMO (management) on how far to push IHC for tissue of origin in carcinoma of unknown primary, plus entity-level grading disagreements (neuroendocrine grading thresholds, prostate grade group versus Gleason). The WHO definition binds the morphology and grade rule; NCCN and ESMO are management overlays. Version skew across WHO volumes (CNS updated 2024 while others remain at their original 5th edition year) is itself pinned per organ site.
  • Breast biomarkers. HER2-low and HER2-ultralow: ESMO 2023 consensus reports HER2-low as a distinct category to guide T-DXd, while ASCO/CAP 2023 and NCCN decline to create new categories. ORE binds ASCO/CAP as primary and treats ESMO HER2-low as a separate explicitly versioned ESMO-sourced rule only when a T-DXd decision requires it. PD-L1 has no harmonized cross-assay scoring standard, so it stays strictly assay-locked to the 22C3 pharmDx FDA companion diagnostic.
  • Molecular and NGS. US tier framework (AMP/ASCO/CAP 2017 plus OncoKB, anchored to FDA and NCCN) versus ESMO ESCAT (six tiers, more conservative about cross-tumor extrapolation). The same alteration can read as Tier IA / OncoKB Level 1 in the US while ESMO holds it at ESCAT I-C or II. ORE records which framework scored the rule and does not blend them.
  • Genomic instability. TMB is the principal disagreement: the FDA grants tissue-agnostic pembrolizumab for TMB-H (at or above 10 mut/Mb by FoundationOne CDx), while ESMO declines a universal pan-tumor cutoff and NCCN often rates TMB category 2B in settings like CUP. HRD must specify the validated companion diagnostic (myChoice CDx), not just HRD-positive status. MSI/MMR is the most concordant.
  • Regimen selection. NCCN versus ESMO, with concordance varying by tumor type (roughly 80 percent colon, about 86 percent breast, near 100 percent non-Hodgkin lymphoma). NCCN tends to list multiple acceptable options tied to US approvals; ESMO is more restrictive and weights the Magnitude of Clinical Benefit Scale; ASTRO can differ on radiation dose and fractionation. ORE binds NCCN as primary, records the ESMO or ASTRO position as a secondary annotation, and surfaces the conflict.
  • Line of therapy. NCCN and ESMO diverge on sequencing even when they agree on active drugs (ADC versus chemotherapy in later-line NSCLC and breast, triplet versus doublet first line in colorectal and pancreatic, transplant and MRD branching in myeloma), and their version cadences fall out of sync, so a new agent may be preferred in one before the other lists it.
  • Risk and prognostic scoring. Node-positive HR-positive HER2-negative disease: post-RxPONDER, NCCN and ASCO endorse the 21-gene score to spare chemotherapy in postmenopausal women with 1 to 3 positive nodes and a score below 26, while ESMO has treated the predictive value in node-positive disease as weaker, and ESMO grants the strongest evidence level only to prospectively tested assays. AJCC and NCCN can also differ on which biomarkers enter prognostic stage versus treatment selection.
  • Dose and schedule. FDA, ASCO, and GOG recommend capping the carboplatin Calvert-formula GFR at 125 mL/min (important as labs move from Jaffe to enzymatic IDMS), while ESMO-aligned data argue capping lacks clinical evidence; US labels lean on Child-Pugh class for hepatic dosing while ESMO and pharmacology literature push bilirubin and transaminase-based or PK-driven adjustment. ORE binds the per-agent number to the USPI, enforces the FDA cap by default with the assay flag, and treats ESMO positions as RAG context that can prompt clinician review.
  • Drug interactions. The FDA label states mechanism and a binary or conditional action but rarely a graded severity; tertiary compendia (Lexicomp, Micromedex) assign their own tiers that disagree with each other and with the label; NCCN supportive care sometimes endorses monitored co-administration where a strict label reading says avoid; ESMO may weight EMA labeling. ORE binds the FDA SPL as the deterministic authority and surfaces the rest as retrieved context, never as an automatic override.
  • Pharmacogenomics. Coverage differs by gene-drug pair (CPIC has a full DPYD guideline but only a level 1A flag for UGT1A1-irinotecan, where DPWG has a full guideline), and dose algorithms differ even where both cover a gene (CPIC activity-score-based versus DPWG variant-specific reductions; DPWG roughly 70 percent irinotecan start versus RNPGx tying reduction to higher dose tiers). ORE pins one body per pair and abstains or escalates when configured sources disagree.
  • Adverse event management. CTCAE only grades severity and prescribes no treatment, so the step must come from NCCN or ESMO; NCCN and ESMO diverge on corticosteroid dosing and taper, the discontinue-versus-hold-and-rechallenge threshold, and aggressiveness of second-line immunosuppression for steroid-refractory colitis, hepatitis, and pneumonitis, with version drift between bodies. ORE binds each rule to one named body and version and does not blend NCCN and ESMO thresholds.
  • Response assessment. No governing-body conflict (NCCN and ESMO defer to RECIST). The divergence is across variants the engine must pick deterministically by tumor type, modality, and regimen: RECIST 1.1 versus iRECIST (new-lesion handling and pseudoprogression), mRECIST and EASL for hepatocellular carcinoma, Lugano with Deauville for lymphoma, RANO for CNS, Choi for GIST on imatinib, and PCWG3 / PSA for prostate bone-predominant disease.
  • Surveillance and survivorship. ESMO favors less intensive, more symptom-directed follow-up and earlier discontinuation while NCCN tends toward more frequent visits and imaging (documented mismatches in nasopharyngeal and testicular cancer); intra-US, ASCO and ASCO/ACS endorsed survivorship guidelines can specify different intervals than the disease-based NCCN guideline. ORE binds US decisions to NCCN, exposes ESMO as the European alternative, and flags disagreements for review.
  • Progression and rebiopsy. NCCN and ESMO agree rebiopsy at progression is recommended but differ at the margins: ESMO's living format often phrases plasma-first or plasma-acceptable earlier while NCCN retains tissue as preferred (to capture histologic and small-cell transformation that liquid biopsy can miss); the NCCN and ESMO living cadences fall out of sync; and strength-of-recommendation grammar differs (NCCN category versus ESMO ESCAT and I to V).
  • Palliative care. Structural and jurisdictional rather than numeric: ESMO splits the domain into separate end-of-life and supportive/palliative documents and frames end of life as the last weeks to months, while NCCN runs one continuous pathway with explicit prognosis bands; ESMO is more restrictive on benzodiazepines for refractory dyspnea; and referral timing differs (ASCO and NCCN push early concurrent referral while some regional guidance still anchors referral to a roughly 6-month prognosis). ORE binds primary symptom and goals-of-care rules to NCCN, the early-referral trigger to ASCO 2024, and reserves ESMO for European context or end-of-life-specific rules, flagging materially different bindings.
  • Trial eligibility. Structural, not NCCN versus ESMO: treatment guidelines define standard-of-care lines, not eligibility, and must not bind an eligibility rule. Real conflict arises between a restrictive protocol as written and the FDA / ASCO-Friends recommendations to broaden criteria (ORE honors the protocol as written and surfaces modernization guidance only as context, never auto-relaxing a criterion), and between the ClinicalTrials.gov registry text and the latest IRB-approved amendment when the registry lags (ORE abstains pending confirmation of the current version).
  • Performance status and organ function. Bodies agree on the ECOG scale definition but diverge on the thresholds that gate therapy: cisplatin renal cutoffs (commonly CrCl at or above 60 mL/min full-dose, 40 to 55 mL/min split-dose, carboplatin substitution as low as 30 mL/min) and the estimating equation (Cockcroft-Gault versus CKD-EPI) change who qualifies; ECOG as a hard gate differs by regimen and ESMO leans more on geriatric assessment (G8) for older adults; and NCCN binds to FDA labels while ESMO may bind to EMA labels. ORE binds one body per decision and records which one, and does not blend cutoffs.
